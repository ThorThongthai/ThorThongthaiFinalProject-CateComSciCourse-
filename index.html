Happiness in Different Countries

For my project I wanted to look at happiness levels in different countries and compare them. To do this, I pulled from the 2021 World Happiness Report. I visualized the happiness of different countries in different ways.

It’s also very important to note that the 2021 World Happiness Report uses self-reported data. This means that happiness was measured by asking individuals to gauge how happy they think they feel. The specific question the report used was this: “Please imagine a ladder, with steps numbered from 0 at the bottom to 10 at the top. The top of the ladder represents the best possible life for you and the bottom of the ladder represents the worst possible life for you. On which step of the ladder would you say you personally feel you stand at this time?” Although the question is well formulated, there is still always an inherent bias to self-reported accounts of phenomena. I thought it was important to acknowledge the limitations of the dataset I pulled from and therefore acknowledge the limitations of my data visualizations as well.

For my first data visualization, I created a world map with shades of the same color to represent Happiness levels in different countries. The darker the color, the happier the country. Grey means there is no data for that country. The advantage to this visualization is that you can see visually which regions of the world are happier than other regions. Comparisons between different continents become really easy. For example, you can see that people in North and South America seem to be happier than people in Africa.

For my second data visualization, I created a horizontal bar chart that ranks all of the countries from most happy to least happy. The x axis is happiness score.

For my third data visualization, I created a bubble chart where the size of the bubble corresponds with a country’s happiness level. The bigger the bubble, the happier the country. I thought that this data visualization was fun and looks cool. The advantage is similar to the bar chart where you can see with a glance which countries are happiest.

Although my visualizations offer compelling ways to compare happiness levels between countries, it doesn’t offer much explanation to the factors that may be contributing to happiness.
